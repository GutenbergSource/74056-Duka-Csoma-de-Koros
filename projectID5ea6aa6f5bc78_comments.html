Alexander Csoma de Körös (Kőrösi Csoma Sándor in Hungarian), was a Hungarian philologist and Orientalist, author of the first Tibetan–English dictionary and grammar book. He was called Phyi-glin-gi-grwa-pa in Tibetan, meaning "the foreign pupil", and was declared a bosatsu or bodhisattva by the Japanese in 1933. (Wikipedia)

This book, by Theodore Duka, a British Army surgeon of Hungarian origin who worked in India describes his live and works. It shows the utmost determination Csoma had in trying to locate (in the end unsuccessfully) the original homeland and source of the Magyar (Hungarian) people and their language.

The book is prepared from relatively bad scans from the Internet Archive (primary source) and the Public Library of India (fall-back source), but should be reasonable to read. Please head to the secondary source if you have trouble reading a few letters in the primary source.

The book will include numerous accents, a few Tibetan words with double capitals at the beginning, which should be retained as such, and some footnotes.

Have fun!

Please do not forget to type the implicit a after the last letter of a word, if it is not explicitly 'cut off' with a virama symbol (otherwise the conversion software will introduce an erroneous virama).

Please be careful to distinguish the ā-vowel sign from the right-side bar in letters such as ण and श (compare ण with णा, [DV: .na] versus [DV: .naa])
